The Process

Disrupting Momentum with Two Hands

One of the most important discoveries during the process was a movement score that eventually became the signature movement in the piece.

The score emerged from my background as a freestyle and hip-hop dancer. When I freestyle, I often look for ways to interrupt my own habits and disrupt patterns before they become predictable. During one improvisation session, I noticed that whenever I wanted to stop myself from continuing a movement pathway, I would place both hands on a part of my body and use that point of contact to redirect myself somewhere else.

I began exploring this idea further and eventually developed a score that I called Disrupting Momentum with Two Hands. (Will be mentioned as “The score” hereafter)

The rules are - Movement continues until both hands arrive at the same point on the body. Once both hands make contact, that point generates a new direction or impulse. The body then follows that impulse until the body is interrupted again. As the score developed, I expanded it beyond the body so that performers could also place both hands at a point in space and allow that imagined point to generate movement.

What interested me most about the score was its adaptability. Although the structure remained the same, the quality of the movement could change completely depending on how it was approached. This meant that the same score could exist throughout the entire choreography while taking on different characteristics in each stage of the cycle.

It was present in creation, preservation, destruction, illusion, and release, but transformed according to the qualities of each section. Looking back, this score became one of the most significant choreographic tools I developed throughout the project because it allowed a single movement principle to evolve alongside the larger ideas of the work.

Space and Audience

The design of the performance space emerged from the image of Natraj surrounded by a circular ring. I became interested in the idea that the audience should not observe the cycle from outside it but should instead be placed within it.

This led me to position the audience in the centre of the space while the performance happened around them.

Initially, I considered placing performers around the audience at all times so that every audience member would constantly have something directly in front of them. However, I became more interested in the opposite possibility. What happens when there is nothing directly in front of someone? What happens when they have to search for the action? How does their behaviour change when attention becomes a choice rather than something directed for them?

These questions became important throughout the process.

Rather than trying to ensure that every audience member saw exactly the same performance, I became interested in creating multiple perspectives. Different audience members would inevitably notice different things depending on where they were sitting and where their attention was drawn. This uncertainty became part of the choreographic structure itself.

Audience behaviour was often very different from what I had anticipated, which I found fascinating. Watching people negotiate their focus and physically respond to movement occurring around them became as interesting to me as the choreography itself. The whole space therefore became an active choreographic element.

The Five Stages

The overall structure of the work was based on the five stages of Natraj’s cosmic dance: creation, preservation, destruction, illusion, and release. However, I was not interested in presenting these as five completely separate chapters. Instead, I wanted them to blend into one another so that the transitions felt continuous, sometimes gradual and sometimes abrupt.

Creation

Creation was where I began investigating ideas of growth and evolution. I became interested in how something large could emerge from something almost imperceptibly small.

Circling became an important movement principle within this section because it reflected the cyclical nature of the work while also allowing movement to expand and develop. Initially, performers began from compact and restricted positions before gradually allowing movement to grow.

During rehearsals, I realised that my instructions were not always producing the clarity I wanted. The circling sometimes became generalised and lost its sense of development. Through discussions with my supervisor and further experimentation, I refined the score so that movement had to travel through the body like a current, slowly spreading from a single point to the whole space encompassing them rather than simply creating circles for their own sake.

The score’s quality in this section was for the momentum to create circles.

Preservation

Preservation emerged from questions about maintenance. Once something has been created, how is it sustained? What keeps a system functioning?

I became interested in repetition, continuity, and collective responsibility. Movement structures explored the idea of maintaining momentum and keeping something alive over time.

Walking became particularly important within this section. I was inspired by Siobhan Davies' Rotor, particularly its use of walking as choreography, which influenced my development of the Preservation section. The focus was not on walking itself but on the effort required to remain connected while constantly adjusting to change.

This section was performed in sounds made by the performers. After the sound of the Damru, I became interested in what happened when the sound disappeared. The silence was not empty but full of the resonance of what had just occurred. Preservation therefore became connected not only to maintaining movement but also to maintaining memory, energy, and attention.

Destruction

Destruction was initially one of the most difficult sections for me to choreograph.

I knew I wanted to draw inspiration from Tandav, the dance associated with Natraj. During rehearsals and discussions with my supervisor, a connection emerged between the twisting actions I was exploring and the physical movement of the instrument itself.

This observation became an important turning point.

The section gradually developed through ideas of twisting, disruption, friction, and collapse. I became interested in destruction not as violence but as transformation through breakdown. The music for this section is a famous Tandav music by Shankar Mahadevan and it became my source of movement. In my practice, I like to listen to the music repeatedly and imagine the piece uncover in front of my eyes. I write these thoughts down and take them to the studio with me to work on. And that is how this section was developed.

The movement score also transformed. The same score that had previously generated growth now became forceful, fragmented, and aggressive. This reinforced the idea that the same systems that create something can also contribute to its destruction.

Illusion

I became interested in contemporary forms of distraction and the ways people can become disconnected from larger realities such as social media, constant information, and digital culture. However, I did not want the choreography to become a direct commentary on these issues.

Instead, I approached illusion as a quality.

I was particularly inspired by the image of Natraj standing upon the figure that represents illusion. This became an important reference point throughout the process. I wanted the movement to feel light, effortless, and almost dreamlike, creating a sense of comfort while simultaneously suggesting a lack of awareness. The score in this section, therefore, was also light and instead of full palm to be used, it only used two fingers.

Release

Tracing emerged as the central choreographic principle within this section. I became interested in the act of noticing, sensing, and reconnecting with the body. The section was designed to feel meditative and reflective, allowing performers to gradually let go of tension accumulated throughout the cycle.

The movement score transformed once again. Instead of disrupting momentum, touch became a means of softening and releasing it. Every contact encouraged relaxation rather than redirection.

The final moments of release intentionally led back towards creation. This return reinforced the idea that there is no true ending to the cycle. Release simply becomes the beginning of something new.

Sound

Sound was as carefully considered as movement throughout the process.

Initially, I wanted to use an Indian folk Damru because of its association with Natraj and creation. However, while searching for the instrument, I struggled to find one that produced the sound I was looking for. This led me to discover the Chöd Damru.

As I researched it further, I became interested in its use within Buddhist meditation practices and it being the sound of Emptiness. The instrument no longer represented only creation. It also introduced ideas of space, absence, and resonance. It fit with the Creation as something present and not present at the same time.

It was played while cutting through the space and around the space constantly. (Chöd means cutting through) Additionally, it created a moving sound which made the audience to choose their attention.

Learning to play the Damru also became part of the process. Over the course of the project, I taught myself enough to perform with it within the work. Although challenging, particularly alongside the demands of choreographing and performing, this became one of the most rewarding aspects of the project.

Problems and Adapting

A performer became injured shortly before the performance, requiring a replacement to learn material within a very limited timeframe. Other performers faced personal circumstances that affected rehearsal attendance. These situations required flexibility and forced me to rethink aspects of the work.

Rather than seeing these challenges as obstacles alone, I began treating them as part of the choreographic process. Additional rehearsals, individual sessions, and adjustments to material became necessary. The work changed because of these realities.

Looking back, the final version of the piece was not exactly the work I imagined at the beginning of the process. However, I now see that transformation as an important part of the project itself.

Costume and Design

The visual design of the work was intentionally simple.

Performers were asked to wear white while the surrounding environment remained black. I was interested in white as a neutral presence. It reflected light, contrasted strongly with the black environment, and created a sense of unity. The performers chose what they were comfortable with and made them feel confident in.

The performance space itself was equally important. The black floor and surrounding darkness supported the visual contrast I wanted to create.

Practical solutions became part of the design process. Rather than purchasing new cushions, I bought black cushion covers and transformed existing cushions to fit the visual environment. Similarly, a simple black rope was the boundary between audience and performers. It gave the audience a clear margin and not be disruptive.

These decisions reflected the wider approach of the project: simple solutions that supported the experience without distracting from it.

The performance presented on 1 June, 2026 is only one version of this choreography. Throughout the process, I continually discovered new possibilities, alternative structures, and different directions the work could take. In many ways, the project remains unfinished.

That feels appropriate for a work inspired by cycles.

There is no true beginning or ending. The choreography, like the ideas that inspired it, continues to evolve. The performance ends with a return to creation because the cycle never stops. The work simply begins again.

Harshita Negi
