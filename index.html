This choreography is an invitation to sit with the idea of not a clear end. Think about how things change but go through the similar stages. The stages vary in duration sometimes and sometimes they stay same for all. You are born, you preserve this life by eating and maintaining the shell you live in, you still are aging and nearing death everyday, you live in the illusion of life and it’s idea of living without believing there’s an end, and you eventually realize and release yourself out of this shell.

A rise of government and it’s creation starts with joint effort and building a constitution. In life there is proof that after it is created it succumbs to corruption in some or the other way (its destruction) before we try to preserve it and try to keep the system working. We live in illusion of it will change someday but finally realize that it was destroyed long ago and it’s time to rebuild it again.

A flower blooms, attracts bees to preserve itself, dries inside and keeps the color to soldier on but eventually falls and goes to the ground again.

There are small, big, significant and insignificant cycles all around that we see and will realize of while you encounter this piece.

This writing was inspired by Chapter 8, Same Difference, by Nicola Conibere in The Creative Critic: Writing as/about Practice (2018). Reading Conibere's text made me think about how choreography can continue to exist through writing, not as documentation, but as another experience of the work. Following that inspiration, the text below is written as an extension of the performance rather than a description of it, inviting the reader to move through the choreography and its cycles through language.

Project Inspiration

Dance entered my life unexpectedly when I accidentally walked into a dance class as a child. One of the first things I learned there was to pay respect to Natraj before and after practice. My teacher, Mr. Raj, taught me the movement for doing it, and for years I followed it without thinking about it much. While developing this project, I found myself returning to that memory and wondering what it actually meant. Why was Natraj so important to dancers? What was it that I had been acknowledging every time I bowed before and after dancing?

As I began researching Natraj, reading translations of Hindu texts and looking at different interpretations of the image, I became increasingly interested in the cosmic dance. What stood out to me was not the idea of Natraj as a deity, but the idea of dance as a continuous cycle of creation, preservation, destruction, illusion, and release. These cycles are often discussed on a cosmic scale, but I became fascinated by the fact that they could also exist within everyday life. They exist within societies, relationships, bodies, thoughts, and even within the atoms that make up our existence.

Rather than creating a work about worship or religion, I wanted to create a work that explored these cycles as something we are already part of. The project became a way for me to investigate how these stages might be experienced physically, spatially, and collectively. As the work developed, it became less about representing Natraj and more about understanding what these cycles meant to me as an artist and choreographer.

Audience interaction has always been central to my choreographic practice. I am interested in how audiences experience performance rather than simply observe it. I often work with structures that encourage audiences to make decisions about where to look, what to focus on, and how they engage with what is happening around them. This project became an opportunity to explore those interests further while investigating the ideas inspired by Natraj.

Harshita Negi
